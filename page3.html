Social Media, Cults, and Online Extremism

What type of person joins a cult?

Young adults in search of their personal identity are more likely to join cults. At the time of cult indoctrination, people are emotionally vulnerable and less likely to have support systems. Many people are looking to better themselves, and cults often market themselves as a way toward self improvement. People who join cults are also in need of a sense of community.

It is important to note that joining a cult is a complex phenomenon influenced by various factors, and people from diverse backgrounds can get involved. Some common factors associated with cult recruitment include:

  • Sense of belonging, purpose, identity, vulnerability
  • Desire for answers or meaning in life

Research suggests that cults often target individuals who may be going through transitional periods in their lives, such as

  • College studentes and recent graduates
  • Individuals experiencing a personal crisis
  • Those seeking spiritual fulfillment

Cult leaders may use manipulative tactics to exploit vulnerabilities and gradually exert control over their followers.

How to avoid Joining Cults and Extremist Groups

  • Educate yourself about online safety, privacy settings, and how social media algorithms work to recognize and resist online manipulation.
  • Have healthy skepticism to help in evaluating claims objectively.
  • Maintain strong connections with friends, family, and communities who can act as a safety net.
  • Verify information before believing and sharing it.
  • Stay informed about cults, extremist ideologies, and their tactics. Knowledge is a powerful tool against indoctrination.

The following are resources useful for those seeking to leave a cult or help someone who is involved.

  1. Books:
    • "Combatting Cult Mind Control" by Steven Hassan
    • "Cults in Our Midst" by Margaret Thaler Singer
  2. Cult Awareness Organizations:
    • Cult Education Institute (CEI) - provides information and resources on cults, mind control, and related topics. Website: Cult Education Institute
    • International Cultic Studies Association (ICSA) - ICSA offers resources for those affected by cults, including conferences and publications. Website: International Cultic Studies Association
  3. Support Groups:
    • Ex-Cult.org offers online forums for survivors to share their experiences
  4. Counseling and Exit Counseling:
    • Cult Education Institute - may help arrange exit counseling sessions, which involve a trained counselor helping a person to evaluate their involvement with a cult critically.
